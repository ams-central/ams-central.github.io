Understanding Antimicrobial Resistance

Explore comprehensive information about antimicrobials, microbes, and their impact on human health. Learn about the challenges of antimicrobial resistance and how we can combat it together.

What Are Antimicrobials?

Medicines used to prevent and treat infections caused by microorganisms in humans, animals, and plants.

Meet the Microbes

Microbes include bacteria, viruses, fungi, and protozoa. These are tiny organisms all around us, often too small to be seen by the naked eye, especially bacteria.

  • Our bodies host a lot of microbes. We even have what we call normal flora, or "good microbes," that help with vital processes like digestion.
  • The most common types of microbes are bacteria, viruses, and fungi, with protozoa also being part of the group.
  • Most of them are microscopic, meaning we can only see them under a microscope. Some fungi, like molds or mushrooms, are visible to the naked eye.

Types of Microbes and Their Treatments

Bacteria

Single-celled organisms that can thrive in various environments water, air, skin, and even inside the human body. Some bacteria cause diseases.

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis causes tuberculosis
  • Escherichia coli, which is naturally found in our gut, has certain strains that can cause urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  • The drugs used to treat bacterial infections are called antibacterials or antibiotics

Viruses

Cannot survive on their own; they need a host. They invade healthy cells and use them to reproduce.

  • Common illnesses caused by viruses include the flu, the common cold, HIV, and COVID-19
  • Important note: antibiotics do not work against viruses. For viral infections, we need antiviral drugs

Fungi

Include molds, yeast, and mushrooms. Some fungi are used to make bread, wine, or beer, and some antibiotics are derived from fungi.

  • Harmful fungi can cause skin infections like ringworm (an-an), jock itch (had-had), and athlete's foot
  • Drugs used to treat fungal infections are called antifungals, often in the form of creams or ointments

Parasites

  • Include lice, worms, and mosquitoes that transmit diseases like malaria
  • Malaria is not caused by bacteria or a virus, but by a parasite carried by mosquitoes

Summary of Antimicrobial Types

To summarize the types of drugs used for each microbe:

  • Antibiotics – for bacterial infections
  • Antivirals – for viral infections
  • Antifungals – for fungal infections
  • Antiparasitics – for parasitic infections like malaria

Understanding AMR (Antimicrobial Resistance)

What is AMR?

  • AMR happens when microbes become resistant to the drugs designed to kill them
  • This makes infections harder, and sometimes impossible, to treat

Why Does AMR Happen?

  • Overprescription of antibiotics
  • Not finishing the full course of medicine
  • Self-medication and prescription sharing
  • Using antibiotics in livestock
  • Poor sanitation and infection control

Myths vs. Facts

Myth: "Antibiotics treat colds."
Fact: Colds are viral; antibiotics don't work on viruses.
Myth: "It's okay to stop antibiotics when I feel better."
Fact: You must finish the full course.
Myth: "Antibiotics are safe to use whenever I feel sick."
Fact: Antibiotics should only be used when prescribed for a specific bacterial infection.

AMR in the Philippines and the World

Current Health Situation in the Philippines (WHO, 2023)

  • New HIV infections (a viral disease) have been increasing since 2022
  • Tuberculosis cases (caused by bacteria) have also been rising
  • Malaria (a parasitic disease) has been improving since 2021
  • Hepatitis B remains steady

The Problem: We Are in a "Discovery Void"

  • We haven't discovered a new class of antimicrobials since lipopeptides in 1987.
  • That's over 30 years without a major breakthrough. And now, even the newly developed drugs face resistance before they even reach the market.


Source: Few antibiotics under development – How did we end up here? – Antibiotic Under Development

AMR Along the Food Chain

Antibiotics are often used not just in humans but also in animals like chickens, pigs, cows, and even fish, not necessarily to treat illness, but to promote faster growth or prevent infections before they occur. However, when antibiotics are overused in animal farming, resistant bacteria can develop in these animals.

These resistant bacteria can then:

  • Enter the food chain – through meat, milk, or other animal products
  • Spread to humans – when food is undercooked, when people handle raw meat, or through contaminated water
  • Spread to the environment – through animal waste used as fertilizer or runoff into rivers and lakes

The Challenge in Our Country

1. Prescription Sharing

This happens when people recommend or give their prescribed antibiotics to friends or family members. Sometimes we leave leftover antibiotics in the fridge and pass them on to someone else, even though that medication was prescribed specifically for us. This is dangerous because what worked for one person might not be appropriate for another.

2. Leaving "Reserve" Medicines

Some people keep leftover antibiotics "just in case" they get sick again in the future. While this might seem like a practical idea, it actually encourages self-medication and improper use, especially if the next illness doesn't require antibiotics at all.

3. Not Finishing Medication

This is when a person stops taking their antibiotics once they feel better, instead of completing the full course. As a result, leftover antibiotics accumulate at home. Studies show that in the Philippines, antibiotics can even be bought from small community stores without a prescription. But remember: antibiotics require a doctor's prescription for a reason they must be taken correctly to be effective and safe.

4. Self medication

It's common for many of us to skip the doctor's visit and just buy medicine on our own, thinking we already know what to take. People self-medicate for many reasons:

  • Fear of side effects
  • Fear of diagnosis
  • High cost of seeing a doctor

What We Can Do

  • Use antibiotics only when prescribed by a healthcare professional
  • Finish the entire course, even if you feel better
  • Never share antibiotics or take someone else's leftover medicine
  • Practice good hygiene
  • Ensure access to clean water and proper sanitation
  • Follow infection control measures in healthcare settings
  • Get vaccinated when needed

Proper Disposal of Expired or Unused Medicines

  • Talk to your pharmacist about how to properly return unused or expired medicines
  • Do not throw antibiotics in sinks or toilets, as they can enter and contaminate our environment
